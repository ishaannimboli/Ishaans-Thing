True Meaning

The first version of ‘Three Blind Mice’ was published in ‘Deuteromelia’ (or ‘The Seconde part of Musicks Melodie’) in 1609, a collection of English tales and songs, composed by English musician Thomas Ravenscroft. This is where the original lyrics derived from.

One speculation of the story’s deep meaning relates to Queen Mary I of England (1516-1558). It is believed that she blinded and executed three Protestant bishops, called the Oxford Martyrs. These could possibly have been the ‘three blind mice’ referred to in the nursery rhyme.

Queen Mary I was the Queen of England and Ireland from July 1553 to her death in November 1558, who achieved the nickname ‘Bloody Mary’, for executing over 300 Protestants. Mary I was a devout Catholic, who wanted to restore Roman Catholicism to England, and viewed anyone who challenged its beliefs (such as Protestants) a committer of treason to God. She used this reasoning to execute hundreds of Protestants: the Marian Persecutions. The persecutions of Protestants lasted 5 years, from 1553 to 1558, and included the Oxford Martyrs.

Queen Mary I

The Oxford Martyrs were three Protestant bishops, tried and burnt at the stake in 1555, on the ground of heresy and treason. The martyrs, bishops Hugh Latimer, Nicholas Ridley and Thomas Cranmer, were supporters of Lady Jane Grey, the Protestant rival for the throne to Mary I. Once Mary I came to power, she persecuted the Protestants who were in favour of Jane Grey, Latimer, Ridley and Cranmer included. The three were tried at University Church of St Mary the Virgin, and burnt at the stake outside the city’s walls, where Broad Street in Oxford is located. Latimer and Ridley were burnt on 16 October 1555, and Cranmer was burnt on 21 March 1556. The Martyrs’ Memorial in Oxford commemorates their martyrdom.

In the modern lyrics, the farmer’s wife is believed to refer to Queen Mary I, who “cut off the tails” of three blind mice, or executed three Protestant bishops. However, there are no history records of the bishops being blinded before their execution. The blindness could possibly symbolise how the bishops were blind to the ‘true message of God’, representing how Mary I believed the Protestants were ‘blind’ to Catholicism.
